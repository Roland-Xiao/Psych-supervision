## 1. Supervision Models Quick Reference

Understanding different supervision models helps therapists select a supervisor whose style fits their needs.

| Supervision model | Core concept | Best for | Key figures |
|------------------|-------------|----------|-------------|
| **Integrated Developmental Model (IDM)** | Adjusts structure and support to match the therapist's developmental stage | Therapists at any stage | Stoltenberg & McNeill |
| **Seven-Eyed Model** | Examines supervisory and clinical material from 7 perspectives | Experienced therapists | Hawkins & Shohet |
| **Discrimination Model** | Supervisor shifts between teacher / counselor / consultant roles | Structured supervision | Bernard |
| **Psychoanalytic Supervision** | Parallel process, transference, and countertransference within supervision | Dynamically oriented therapists | Frawley-O'Dea & Sarnat |
| **Competency-Based Supervision (CBS)** | Evaluates therapist development against explicit competency standards | Training programs | Falender & Shafranske |
| **Reflective Supervision** | Emphasizes supervisor's self-reflection and relational engagement | Advanced development | Watkins |
| **Group Supervision** | Uses group dynamics for multi-perspective feedback; cost-effective | Resource-limited environments | — |

### Seven-Eyed model overview (Hawkins & Shohet)

Attention can shift across these 7 lenses during any supervision session:

```
Eye 1  →  Content presented by the client (the facts)
Eye 2  →  Therapist interventions and strategies
Eye 3  →  The relationship between therapist and client
Eye 4  →  The therapist's internal responses (countertransference)
Eye 5  →  The supervisory relationship itself
Eye 6  →  The supervisor's internal responses
Eye 7  →  The broader systemic and cultural context
```

1. **CPS Registration System** — registered supervisor directory available on their website
2. **Regional Mental Health Association referrals**

5. **Clinical psychology faculty at universities** — some offer external supervision

## 5. Online Supervision and Digital Platforms

### Video supervision

Video supervision is now widely accepted and supported by research on effectiveness.

**Appropriate when:**

- A competent supervisor is not geographically accessible
- The therapist seeks expert supervision in a specific modality
- Time zone differences permit access to international English-language supervision

**Key considerations:**

- Verify the supervisor's credentials (modality training background, supervisory hours, licensure)
- Clarify confidentiality agreements (data security of the video platform)
- Not suitable for real-time discussion of extremely high-risk cases requiring immediate intervention

### Key platforms

| Platform | Language | Notes |
|---------|---------|-------|
| **Jiandanxinli supervision** | Chinese | Largest domestic counseling platform; supervision matching |
| **Zoom / Tencent Meeting** | Bilingual | For use after independently contacting a supervisor |
| **CAPA** | English / bilingual | Psychoanalytic orientation; serving mainland therapists for years |
| **Psychology Today** | English | US supervisor directory |
| **Therapy Den** | English | Multicultural-oriented supervisors |

### Building a peer supervision group

When formal supervision resources are limited, peer supervision groups are a vital supplement.

**Steps to establish:**

1. Find 3–6 therapists at a similar developmental stage
2. Decide on meeting frequency *(every 2–4 weeks recommended)*
3. Establish structure: case presentation format, time allocation, confidentiality agreement
4. Periodically invite an experienced supervisor for group sessions to reduce cost

**Structured group format (60–90 minutes):**

```
Opening                5 min   →  Check in on everyone's current state
Case presentation     15–20 min →  Structured narrative (stay within time)
Open discussion       20–30 min →  Multi-perspective feedback
Summary and closing   10 min   →  Supervision closing question
```

## 7. Supervision Fees and Accessibility Strategies

### Mainland China market reference (2024)

| Supervision type | Fee range (RMB / hour) |
|----------------|----------------------|
| Individual supervision — junior supervisor | ¥300–600 |
| Individual supervision — mid-level registered supervisor | ¥600–1,200 |
| Individual supervision — senior / internationally trained | ¥1,200–3,000+ |
| Group supervision — per-person share | ¥100–400 |
| Online international supervision (English) | $100–300 USD |

> *Fee is not a proxy for quality — the key is building an effective working alliance with the supervisor.*

### Strategies for reducing supervision costs

1. **Group supervision** — 4–6 people share the cost, reducing individual expense to 1/4–1/6
2. **Training programs with built-in supervision** — When enrolling, prioritize programs that include supervised practice hours
3. **Peer supervision + periodic expert consultation** — Peer supervision for routine cases; paid expert supervision for complex cases
4. **In-house institutional supervision** — Advocate for supervision resources from your workplace (hospital, NGO, etc.)
5. **Student / trainee fee reductions** — Some supervisors offer reduced fees for graduate students
6. **Supervision exchange** — Therapists of comparable level supervising each other (formalized peer supervision)

## 8. Self-Supervision Tools

When external supervision is not immediately available, these tools help therapists maintain reflective practice.

### Case reflection log template

```
Session date:
Client code:
The single most important thing I noticed today:
My bodily response during the session:
What did I avoid?
The decision I felt least certain about:
If my supervisor were present, what would they ask me?
What did this case bring up from my own life?
One thing I want to do differently in the next session:
```

### Seven-Eyed self-check

Answer in sequence:

1. What was the client saying? *(content)*
2. What did I do? *(intervention)*
3. What was happening between us? *(relational dynamics)*
4. What was my internal response? *(countertransference)*
5. What does this case evoke in the supervisory relationship? *(parallel process)*
6. What is my own current state? *(self-reflection)*
7. How do larger systems (culture / institution / society) affect this case?

### Recommended frequency for reflective writing

| Caseload | Recommended frequency |
|---------|----------------------|
| 1–5 sessions per week | One overall reflection at end of week |
| 5–15 sessions per week | Record immediately after each difficult session |
| 15+ sessions per week | Brief daily note (5 min); detailed weekly reflection |

## 9. Vicarious Trauma and Professional Support

### Recognition signals

When the following are present, supervisory work should prioritize the therapist's own state before the case:

- Losing curiosity or empathy toward certain client types
- Thinking about clients repeatedly outside working hours
- World view darkening (*"therapy doesn't work," "people are all like this"*)
- Difficulty recovering after sessions — unable to return from a high-arousal state
- Nightmares, or intrusive imagery connected to clients' disclosures
- Relationship with family or partner affected

### Crisis support resources (China mainland)

| Resource | Contact |
|---------|---------|
| **Beijing Suicide Research and Prevention Center** | 010-82951332 |
| **National Mental Health Support Line** | 400-161-9995 |
| **Lifeline China** | 400-821-1215 |
| **Peer support** — informal professional network | — |

> Seeking psychological support as a therapist is not weakness — it is professional responsibility.
>
> **Recommendation:** All therapists working long-term with trauma maintain their own regular personal therapy (1–4 sessions per month).

### Professional sustainability recommendations

```
Minimum configuration (resource-limited):
  ✓  At least 1 supervision session per month (individual or group)
  ✓  At least 1 personal therapy session or peer support contact per month
  ✓  Clear "offline time" each week — no client contact, no case thinking

Ideal configuration:
  ✓  Weekly supervision (when seeing high case volumes)
  ✓  2–4 personal therapy sessions per month
  ✓  1–2 professional training opportunities per year
  ✓  Connection with a professional peer community
  ✓  Regular physical self-care (exercise, sleep)
```
