# Countertransference: In-Depth Reference

> **Load condition:** When the therapist describes an intense emotional reaction to a client, or explicitly raises countertransference confusion.

---

## I. Theoretical Evolution

### Freud's original definition (1910)

Countertransference = the therapist's unconscious reaction to the client's transference — a **"contamination" to be eliminated**.

→ The therapist should function as a "blank screen," clearing countertransference through personal therapy.

### The modern integrative definition (Heimann, 1950 — present)

Countertransference = **all** of the therapist's emotional, cognitive, and bodily responses to the client, regardless of origin.

→ Countertransference is **the most valuable clinical data available**, not an obstacle to be removed.

**Why this shift matters:** A therapist's boredom, exhaustion, anger, sexual attraction, or urge to flee — these are not signs of weakness or professional failure. They are the impressions the client leaves on the therapist.

---

## II. Five Types of Countertransference

### 1. Complementary countertransference

**Definition:** Through projective identification, the client induces the therapist to enact a role in their internal relational script.

**Mechanism:** The client's internalized relational template is "authority figure–victim." The therapist begins to feel an impulse to criticize or control the client.

**Clinical value:** Reveals the client's relational pattern — the role the therapist is pulled into is the role of a recurring "other" in the client's life.

**Identifying question:** *"I'm noticing an urge to _______ (criticize / protect / rescue / give up) — is this the role of some important person from this client's life?"*

---

### 2. Concordant countertransference

**Definition:** The therapist feels an emotion that **the client cannot yet express or access.**

**Mechanism:** The client describes a loss in a calm, detached tone, while the therapist feels a wave of grief. That grief does not belong to the therapist — it belongs to the suppressed part of the client.

**Clinical value:** Reflects the client's unconscious state; sometimes more truthful than the client's words.

**Identifying question:** *"Is what I'm feeling right now something this client has not yet been able to feel?"*

---

### 3. Subjective countertransference

**Definition:** Reactions originating from the therapist's personal history and unresolved material.

**Mechanism:** The therapist has their own abandonment trauma. When the client describes a separation, the therapist's response is colored by personal fear.

**Clinical value:** This belongs in **personal therapy**, not supervision. But recognizing it is critical to prevent it from distorting clinical judgment.

**Identifying question:** *"If I bring this feeling to other clients — do others trigger this in me too? If yes, it's more likely my own material."*

---

### 4. Somatic countertransference

**Definition:** Bodily reactions in the therapist — drowsiness, chest tightness, nausea, racing heart, headache.

**Mechanism:** The body receives interpersonal signals earlier than the conscious mind. A therapist who repeatedly feels drowsy with a particular client may not be sleep-deprived — the client may be transmitting a kind of numbness or deadness.

**Clinical value:** The body functions as a perceptual instrument, especially valuable with clients who have poor verbal access to their experience or who are highly dissociative.

**Identifying question:** *"What is happening in my body right now? Does this feeling only occur with this client?"*

---

### 5. Fantasy-based countertransference

**Definition:** The therapist's daydreams, associations, and fantasies about the client — including sexual fantasies, rescue fantasies, or anger fantasies.

**Clinical value:** Clues to unconscious communication, but also a high-risk zone (especially anything sexual).

**Principles for handling:**

- Sexual fantasy ≠ action, but requires honest discussion in supervision
- What does the content of the fantasy (rescue? retaliation? merger?) reveal about the relational dynamic?
- Persistent fantasies call for personal therapy

## III. Four-Step Method for Using Countertransference

```
Step 1 — Sense
  Observe without judgment: "What is happening inside me / in my body right now?"
  Do not rush to explain — observe first.

Step 2 — Distinguish
  "Does this come more from me (personal history), or from the client?"
  Quick test: Bring this feeling to other clients — is it still there?
  Proportions are rarely 0/100 — more often 30/70 or similar.

Step 3 — Understand
  If it primarily comes from the client:
    "What experience is the client trying to evoke in me?"
    "How does this connect to their relational history?"
    "Are they communicating something they cannot put into words?"

Step 4 — Use
  A. Internal use:   Adjust your understanding and response without disclosing
  B. Disclosure:     Share directly with the client (with care; see criteria below)
  C. Personal work:  Recognize it as your own material; bring it to personal therapy
```

## IV. When to Disclose Countertransference

### Conditions for disclosure (all must be met)

- The therapist is confident it **primarily originates from the client**, not a personal reaction (≥70% from the client)
- The client has sufficient **ego strength** to receive it
- The **alliance is solid** (generally at least 10+ sessions in)
- The disclosure is **in the client's interest**, not to relieve the therapist's tension
- The client is **currently within the window of tolerance** (not in a crisis state)

### Language examples for disclosure

- *"While listening to you, I noticed something in myself... I wonder what that might mean for you?"*
- *"Right now I'm feeling a kind of... I'd like to explore whether you're experiencing something similar."*
- *"When you said that just now, I had a brief reaction — I want to name it because I think it may be connected to you..."*

### Situations where disclosure is never appropriate

- Sexual attraction or romantic fantasy *(even when "processed," do not tell the client directly)*
- Intense anger or contempt *(must first be worked through in supervision or personal therapy)*
- When the client is in crisis or functioning at a very low level
- When disclosure is primarily about relieving the therapist's own anxiety

## V. Common Countertransference Patterns and Traps

### Rescue fantasy

**Presentation:** The therapist is overly invested — running over time, lowering fees, taking emergency calls, treating the client as "special."

**Source:** The therapist's omnipotent fantasy, or the client's idealizing transference.

**Risk:** Erosion of boundaries, therapist burnout, client developing dependency.

**Supervision work:** *Why does this therapist need to be needed by this client?*

---

### Complementary anger

**Presentation:** The therapist feels inexplicably irritated, wants to argue with the client, feels unsettled after sessions.

**Source:** The client's projected aggression, or the client inducing the therapist to experience the client's own victimhood.

**Supervision work:** *What is this anger protecting? In the relational script, who is the therapist being cast as?*

---

### Empathic overload

**Presentation:** The therapist feels heavy after sessions, cannot "put down" the client's story, has intrusive imagery.

**Source:** Vicarious transmission of traumatic content.

**Risk:** Early signal of vicarious trauma.

**Supervision work:** Establish a "de-traumatizing" ritual; review overall caseload.

---

### Intellectualization as escape

**Presentation:** The therapist talks extensively about theory, avoiding emotional contact.

**Source:** The therapist's defense (fear of being overwhelmed by the client's emotions), or client-induced intellectualization.

**Supervision work:** *"When you were talking about theory just now, what was happening in your body?"*
