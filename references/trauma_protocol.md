# Trauma Treatment Supervision Protocol

> **Load condition:** When the therapist describes a client with a trauma history, trauma symptoms (flashbacks / dissociation / nightmares / hyperarousal), or when signs of decompensation or dropout risk emerge during treatment.

---

## I. Herman's Three-Stage Model

### Stage 1 — Safety and stabilization *(the foundation)*

**Goal:** Establish a sufficiently safe internal and external foundation.

**Work includes:**

- Building the therapeutic alliance
- Emotional regulation skills (TIPP / mindfulness / safe place technique)
- Activating internal resources (safe space imagery / supportive figures)
- Identifying and expanding the window of tolerance
- Life stabilization (safe environment, physical health, support systems)

**Evaluating readiness to move to Stage 2:**

- [ ] Client can self-regulate back to baseline after emotional activation
- [ ] Alliance is solid (client trusts and is willing to be honest)
- [ ] Client has at least one "safe resource" available on demand
- [ ] Basic daily functioning is maintained (work / relationships / self-care)

> ⚠️ Do not enter Stage 2 until the above conditions are substantially met.

---

### Stage 2 — Mourning and processing

**Goal:** Integrate traumatic memories; reduce trauma symptoms.

**Applicable techniques:**

- EMDR *(first choice; extensive evidence base)*
- Narrative Exposure Therapy (NET)
- Prolonged Exposure (PE)
- Cognitive Processing Therapy (CPT)
- Somatic Experiencing (SE) *(addresses the bodily dimension)*

**Monitoring during the work:**

- [ ] Is the client within the window of tolerance? *(Pause if hyperaroused or hypoaroused)*
- [ ] Is between-session functioning maintained? *(Work, sleep, relationships)*
- [ ] Has self-harm / suicide risk increased?

**Decompensation signals — return immediately to Stage 1:**

- Worsening dissociation
- Collapse of daily functioning
- Onset or increase of self-harm urges
- Alliance rupture
- Client considering dropping out

---

### Stage 3 — Reconnection and integration

**Goal:** Post-trauma reconstruction of self; re-engagement with life.

**Work includes:**

- Rebuilding identity ("I am not only a trauma survivor")
- Restoring relational capacity
- Meaning-making (the possibility of post-traumatic growth)
- Natural conclusion of treatment

---

## II. Window of Tolerance

```
        ┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐
        │            Hyperarousal Zone             │
        │  Panic / Dissociation / Flooding /       │
        │  Flashbacks                              │
        │  → Cannot integrate; cannot learn        │
        ├─────────────────────────────────────────┤
        │                                          │
        │         ★  Window of Tolerance  ★        │
        │                                          │
        │  Sufficient activation to approach       │
        │  trauma material — with enough           │
        │  regulation to avoid being overwhelmed   │
        │  → Processing and new learning happen    │
        │    here                                  │
        │                                          │
        ├─────────────────────────────────────────┤
        │            Hypoarousal Zone              │
        │  Numbness / Detachment / Freeze /        │
        │  Dissociative shutdown                   │
        │  → Cannot integrate; cannot learn        │
        └─────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Supervision application:**

- Assess at every session: Which zone is the client currently in?
- Intervention too intense → client enters hyperarousal (needs downregulation)
- Insufficient support → client enters hypoarousal (needs gentle activation)
- Therapists have their own window of tolerance — those processing heavy trauma material also need supervisory attention

## III. Common Trauma Supervision Scenarios

### Scenario A — Therapist rushes into trauma content

**Signals:**

- *"He/she finally opened up — we went into the trauma details"*
- Therapist feels excited or relieved
- Client begins describing details, then dissociates or suddenly falls silent

**Supervisory response:**

- First ask: Has Stage 1 work been completed? Is the alliance solid?
- The timing to enter trauma content is determined by the *client's* readiness, not the therapist's
- The pace is set by the client; the therapist's role is to follow, not to push

---

### Scenario B — Client dissociates during session

**Recognition signals:**

- Glazed eyes, flat voice
- Sudden shift to third person (*"that girl..."*)
- Stops responding, or responses become mechanical
- Body freezes

**Immediate intervention:**

1. Stop the trauma content; do not continue probing
2. Use voice and presence to bring the client back to the present: *"Can you see me right now?"*
3. Grounding techniques: ask the client to name 5 visible objects / feel their feet on the floor
4. After stabilizing, ask what just happened — do not return to trauma content
5. Assess whether to continue the session or end early

**Supervisory questions:**

- *"Did you notice when the dissociation began?"*
- *"When the client dissociated, what was happening in your body?"*
- *"Did the two of you move too fast in this session?"*

---

### Scenario C — Therapist overwhelmed by trauma material (vicarious trauma)

**Signals:**

- Unable to "let go" after sessions; the client's story continues to intrude
- Sleep is affected (nightmares or insomnia)
- World view darkening: feeling that people are fundamentally bad, that therapy doesn't work
- Losing curiosity about other clients

**Supervision priority:** This session must address the therapist first — not the case.

**Supervisory intervention:**

- Normalize: vicarious trauma is an occupational cost, not a personal weakness
- Ask: What proportion of the caseload consists of trauma cases? Is there sufficient "de-traumatizing" space?
- Suggest: Build a ritualized "transitional space" between intense trauma sessions (washing hands / walking / 5 minutes of stillness)
- If severe: temporarily reduce trauma caseload; seek individual supervision or personal therapy

---

### Scenario D — Client at high risk during trauma exposure

**Immediate assessment:**

- Is this the result of moving into Stage 2 too quickly?
- Is the client's support system still intact?
- Is self-harm functioning as a regulation tool or as a suicide equivalent?

**Response:**

1. Pause trauma exposure work; return to Stage 1
2. Re-evaluate safety status *(see `scripts/risk_assessment.py`)*
3. Increase session frequency or strengthen support contacts
4. Notify supervisor; document actions taken

---

## IV. Therapist Competency Requirements for Trauma Work

The following techniques require specialized training and **cannot be practiced based on reading alone:**

| Technique | Minimum requirement |
|-----------|---------------------|
| EMDR | Certified training (minimum 80 foundational hours + supervised practice) |
| Somatic Experiencing (SE) | Years of systematic training |
| AEDP | Specialized certification |

> **AI supervision note:** If a therapist has not received the above training but is working with trauma clients, the supervisory priority must shift first to: whether this client is appropriate for this therapist's current competency level, and whether referral is necessary.
