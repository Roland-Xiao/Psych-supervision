# Supervision Example: Novice Therapist Anxiety (High-Risk Client)

---

## The Therapist's Presentation

> "I'm a trainee counselor — less than a year of experience. I recently took on a client with suicidal ideation. I'm scared. I feel intensely anxious before every session, afraid of saying the wrong thing. My supervisor told me I'm being overly worried and to relax — but I'm still tense. How am I supposed to manage this anxiety?"

---

## Supervisory Response

Let's pause here.

Your supervisor told you to "relax" — I understand the good intention behind that. But I want to tell you something: **feeling anxious when working with a high-risk client is normal. It's even important.** Complete absence of anxiety would be more concerning, because it would suggest you haven't fully registered the weight of the risk.

So first: your anxiety is not a weakness. It is evidence that you are taking this work seriously.

---

Now I want to understand what's actually inside that anxiety — because "anxiety" is a broad word that can contain quite different things.

What exactly are you afraid of?

- That one wrong sentence might push the client toward an impulsive act?
- That you won't be able to accurately assess the real level of risk?
- That if a crisis happens, you won't know what to do?
- Or that if something happens to this client, the responsibility will fall on you?

---

*(Assuming the therapist says: mainly worried about not being able to assess real risk, and not knowing what to do in a crisis)*

---

Both of those concerns are very specific — and both have workable directions.

**On risk assessment:**

Suicide risk assessment is not "going on instinct." There are concrete dimensions to evaluate:

1. **Intent** — How much does the client want to die? *(Distinguish between "wanting to end the pain" and "wanting to die" — the former expresses a desire to escape suffering, not necessarily a will toward death)*
2. **Plan** — Is there a specific method, time, and place in mind?
3. **Means** — Can the client realistically access that method? Any history of prior attempts?

After each session, ask yourself these three questions. This isn't about diagnosing — it's about having a framework so you're not groping in the dark.

Also pay attention to protective factors: children, religious belief, any future orientation, investment in the therapy itself — all of these matter.

**On crisis response:**

Does your institution have a crisis intervention protocol? Do you know — concretely — who to contact if a client expresses imminent suicidal intent during a session?

This is not something supervision can substitute for. You need to clarify that protocol with your institution directly. If the institution doesn't have one, that itself is a problem that needs to be solved.

---

**On your supervisor telling you to "relax":**

I'm not here to question your supervisor — but I want to ask: in that supervision session, did you feel understood?

Sometimes the supervisory relationship itself is worth examining. If you bring a genuine concern to supervision and feel it's been brushed aside, you may become less willing to open up the next time you hit a difficulty.

Does your supervisor have full awareness that you are carrying a high-risk client? Has this specific case been discussed together?

---

*What this example demonstrates:*
*Not simply echoing "relax" → making the anxiety specific and concrete → providing a risk assessment framework (giving structure) → identifying gaps in institutional support → attending to whether the supervisory relationship itself feels safe.*
