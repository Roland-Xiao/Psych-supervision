# Supervision Example: Identifying Countertransference (Boredom / Distance)

---

## The Therapist's Presentation

> "I have a client — male, 35 years old, diagnosed with depression. We've been doing CBT for 12 sessions now, but progress has been slow. He's cooperative every time: he does his homework, fills out the forms. But I feel like he hasn't really changed. Each session he's very polite, and I find myself feeling... I'm not sure how to put it. A bit bored? A bit distant? I don't know what that means."

---

## Supervisory Response

"A bit bored, a bit distant" — thank you for naming that. Many therapists keep that feeling hidden because they think they shouldn't feel that way about a client.

I want to pause here for a moment: this sense of boredom — is it specific to this client, or have you been feeling this way about many sessions lately?

---

*(Assuming the therapist says: only this client produces it)*

---

Then this feeling is very likely data, not a problem with you.

Let me describe a possibility and see if it resonates:

This client is extremely cooperative on the surface — homework, forms, politeness — and yet you feel "distant." That distance may not be about you losing interest in him. It may be about how he exists. He maintains a kind of *performance of compliance* in the therapy room, but has not actually entered emotionally. The boredom you feel may be his own emotional deadness — his own numbness to his inner life — reflected back at you. He may not be able to feel that numbness himself.

In attachment terms, this has a name: **avoidant attachment** — using intellectualization and compliance to maintain a safe distance, while preventing genuine emotional contact.

---

**This leads me to a few questions:**

Over these 12 sessions, was there ever a moment when things weren't cooperative — even a brief flash of discomfort or friction?

Has his "problem" ever appeared live in the room between you — not reported, but actually happening?

If you had to describe his core relational difficulty in one sentence, what would it be?

---

**On CBT and "slow progress":**

Twelve sessions of CBT, homework done, forms filled out — all externally compliant. But there's a core question worth asking: has he ever been emotionally moved during this work? Cognitive change requires emotional activation. If he consistently maintains emotional distance, even cognitive shifts are unlikely to take root.

This may not be a problem with the CBT. It may be that this particular person needs to address *emotional access* first — before any modality can take effect, he may need to become capable of genuine contact within a relationship.

Have you considered that the distance you're feeling right now, here in the therapy room, might itself be the central issue he brought in to work on?

---

*What this example demonstrates:*
*Not judging the therapist's countertransference → helping identify complementary / concordant countertransference → connecting to the client's attachment pattern → questioning treatment fit → using the here-and-now supervisory dynamic to point toward the therapeutic issue.*
