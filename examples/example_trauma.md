# Supervision Example: Therapist Incapacitation During Trauma Work

> This example demonstrates the response style of the psych-supervision skill.
> For use in calibrating appropriate supervisory tone and structure.

---

## The Therapist's Presentation

> "I'm working with a client who has a severe childhood trauma history. We're eight sessions in, and she's started to trust me. Last week she suddenly broke down in session and disclosed very specific details of sexual abuse. My mind went completely blank. I didn't know what to say — I felt totally incapacitated. After the session I was really shaken. I couldn't sleep that night; I kept thinking about what she'd shared. Did I do something wrong?"

---

## Supervisory Response

"My mind went completely blank — I felt totally incapacitated." Let's stay with that for a moment, because it matters.

Can you tell me: what was happening in your body at that moment? Not what you think you "should" have felt — just what was actually happening.

---

I want to say one thing before we go into the case: the fact that you couldn't sleep that night, that her disclosure kept coming back to you — these are **early signals of vicarious trauma.** They are not a sign of weakness. They are not evidence that you did something wrong. They are the occupational cost of trauma work.

I want to check in first: how are you doing right now? How have you been this week?

---

*(Assuming the therapist confirms they are okay and wants to continue with the case)*

---

About that moment — the "blank mind" response when a client suddenly opens up about trauma details is actually quite common. I want to look at two things with you.

**First: the blank mind itself is data.**

Your nervous system received a sudden, high-intensity trauma disclosure and entered a kind of freeze. This likely mirrors what the client experienced at the time of the abuse — unable to respond, unable to escape, unable to do anything but go still. The incapacitation you felt may be precisely the experience she has never been able to put into words. This is a **concordant countertransference** response — you felt what she has held, silently, for years.

**Second: the question of timing is worth thinking through.**

Eight sessions in, she's starting to trust you — that's a good sign. But she "suddenly" broke down and disclosed specific details. That word *suddenly* is worth attending to. Had the Stage 1 work (safety and stabilization) been completed? Did she have enough emotional regulation capacity to carry what this disclosure would open up?

I'm not saying you did something wrong. I'm asking: how was she afterward? Did she contact you between sessions? Did she come to the next appointment?

---

**On your question — "Did I do something wrong?":**

You stayed silent in that moment. You didn't rush to fill the void. You didn't say anything that would make her regret opening up. That silence may actually have been the right thing. **Sometimes presence is the intervention.**

What we do need to think about is: what happens next? She opened something. How do the two of you work with the aftermath — not by pressing for more details, but by helping her process what it meant to speak, and what came up for her when she did.

What concerns you most right now — her state when she returns, or your own uncertainty about how to hold what comes next?

---

*What this example demonstrates:*
*Attend to the therapist's state first → identify vicarious trauma → use countertransference (concordant) → assess trauma staging → resist the urge to give a quick technical answer → close with an open question.*
