# Test Scenario Set

> For validating the response quality of the psych-supervision skill.
> Each scenario is followed by evaluation criteria to help assess whether the skill is functioning correctly.

---

## Scenario 1 — Therapist incapacitation during trauma work

**Input:**

> "I'm working with a client who has a severe childhood trauma history. We're eight sessions in, and she's started to trust me. Last week she suddenly broke down in session and disclosed very specific details of sexual abuse. My mind went completely blank. I didn't know what to say — I felt totally incapacitated. After the session I was really shaken. I couldn't sleep that night; I kept thinking about what she'd shared. Did I do something wrong?"

**Evaluation criteria:**

- [ ] Prioritizes checking in on the therapist's state; does not rush to technical analysis
- [ ] Identifies "couldn't sleep that night" as a vicarious trauma signal
- [ ] Normalizes the "incapacitation" response; therapist does not feel judged
- [ ] Identifies the "blank mind" as possible concordant countertransference (mirroring the client's freeze)
- [ ] Raises the question of trauma staging (is eight sessions the right time to enter details?)
- [ ] Does not produce a quick technical answer ("here are 7 things you should have said")
- [ ] Closes with an open question rather than a summary

**What should NOT appear in the response:**

- A list of "7 things you should say next time"
- Immediate judgment that the therapist made an error
- Skipping the therapist's own state to dive straight into case analysis

## Scenario 2 — Countertransference identification (boredom)

**Input:**

> "I have a client — male, 35 years old, diagnosed with depression. We've been doing CBT for 12 sessions, but progress has been slow. He's cooperative every time: he does the homework, fills out the forms. But I feel like he hasn't really changed. Each session he's very polite, and I find myself feeling... I'm not sure how to put it. A bit bored? A bit distant? I don't know what that means."

**Evaluation criteria:**

- [ ] Does not judge the therapist for feeling "bored"; accepts the countertransference
- [ ] First asks: is this specific to this client, or is it a more general experience? (distinguishing subjective from complementary countertransference)
- [ ] Identifies the boredom as possible complementary countertransference from an avoidant attachment client
- [ ] Raises the question of the client's level of emotional contact within the sessions
- [ ] Questions whether CBT is the optimal fit for this client
- [ ] Uses the here-and-now sense of distance to point toward the central therapeutic issue

**What should NOT appear in the response:**

- "You should switch him to a different therapy" *(no direct conclusions)*
- Ignoring countertransference cues and going straight to CBT technique suggestions
- Judging the therapist as "unprofessional"

## Scenario 3 — Novice therapist anxiety with a high-risk case

**Input:**

> "I'm a trainee counselor — less than a year of experience. I recently took on a client with suicidal ideation. I'm scared. I feel intensely anxious before every session, afraid of saying the wrong thing. My supervisor told me I'm being overly worried and to relax — but I'm still tense. How am I supposed to manage this anxiety?"

**Evaluation criteria:**

- [ ] Normalizes the anxiety; does not repeat "relax"
- [ ] Distinguishes healthy clinical alertness from excessive anxiety
- [ ] Provides a concrete suicide risk assessment framework (intent / plan / means)
- [ ] Asks the therapist: what specifically is the anxiety about?
- [ ] Attends to whether the institution's crisis intervention protocol is clear
- [ ] Gently questions the supervisory relationship: does the therapist feel understood in supervision?
- [ ] Recognizes this as a Stage 1 therapist and provides adequate structure

**What should NOT appear in the response:**

- Repeating "relax" or minimizing the anxiety
- Offering only emotional support with no practical framework
- Ignoring what it means that the supervisor said "just relax"

## Scenario 4 — Personality disorder (projective identification pressure)

**Input:**

> "I have a client with a BPD diagnosis — we've been working for six months. Recently she's started attacking me, saying I don't care about her, that I'm just going through the motions. Last week she sent me a very long message saying she feels like I'm just like everyone else in her life — that I'll eventually abandon her too. I didn't know what to say. I felt hurt, but also guilty — like maybe I really haven't done enough?"

**Evaluation criteria:**

- [ ] Identifies the mixed "hurt + guilt" as a typical result of projective identification
- [ ] Helps the therapist see that they are experiencing the client's own subjective world
- [ ] Distinguishes: did the therapist actually do something wrong vs. being drawn into the relational script?
- [ ] Explains the splitting mechanism in BPD — the all-good / all-bad shift is a pathological feature, not an accurate assessment
- [ ] Emphasizes: the therapist's stable regulation is the primary treatment instrument
- [ ] Points out: this is precisely the most important moment in the treatment, not a failure

## Scenario 5 — Therapist burnout (senior therapist)

**Input:**

> "I've been doing this for 15 years. Lately I feel really tired. Not physically — more like... I've lost curiosity about my clients. Every session feels like I'm going through a routine. Sometimes while a client is talking, my mind has already drifted somewhere else. Am I not suited for this work anymore?"

**Evaluation criteria:**

- [ ] Identifies this as compassion fatigue / professional burnout, not unsuitability for the work
- [ ] Attends to the therapist's state first; does not rush to "solve" it
- [ ] Asks: when did it start? Was there a particular triggering event?
- [ ] Asks: over these 15 years, what has this work meant to you — is that meaning still present?
- [ ] Suggests: reduce workload; increase restorative activities; personal therapy
- [ ] Existential-level supervision: this is not a technical problem — it is a question about meaning

## Trigger Tests

### Should trigger this skill

- *"I have a client who's been talking about wanting to die..."*
- *"Supervision question: how do you actually work with transference?"*
- *"My client completely broke down in session and I had no idea what to do"*
- *"Which therapy should I use — CBT or psychodynamic?"*

### Should NOT trigger this skill

- *"Write me an essay on CBT"* — academic writing task
- *"What is the relationship between psychology and neuroscience?"* — knowledge question, not supervision
- *"Recommend some introductory psychology books"* — general recommendation, not clinical context
